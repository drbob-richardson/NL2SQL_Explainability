\begin{abstract}
Deciding whether to trust an LLM-generated SQL query requires an estimate of whether it is correct.
We study which signals provide that estimate on hard multi-table text-to-SQL (BIRD), comparing them
on equal footing with bootstrapped confidence intervals. The black-box statistical signals cluster
together and plateau: string, structural, and execution self-consistency, along with structural
priors and semantic schema-linking confidence, all fall between about $0.61$ and $0.68$ \auroc{},
with string self-consistency the strongest at $0.675$. White-box log-probability does not exceed
them ($0.67$), and it adds nothing significant when combined with the best baseline. The signals
that move past this ceiling are verification-based: an LLM judge scores from $0.72$ (GPT-4o-mini) to
$0.78$ (Claude). Because judges from different providers make different errors (GPT-4o and Claude
scores correlate at only $0.43$), a two-provider ensemble reaches $0.82$ \auroc{} with a
well-calibrated probability (ECE $0.03$), and it supports useful abstention frontiers (for example,
$27\%$ coverage at $24\%$ selective risk) where self-consistency offers no valid low-risk subset.
The pattern replicates across two generators and two judge providers. We also ask whether a verifier
can be trained. Fine-tuned verifiers, both encoder and generative, reach about $0.77$ to $0.79$
in-distribution but fall to about $0.66$ on unseen schemas; cross-schema transfer appears to depend
on model scale and reasoning rather than on fine-tuning. In practice, correctness uncertainty for
text-to-SQL lives in reasoning-based signals. A fine-tuned verifier is a good per-deployment tool,
while a verifier that generalizes across schemas currently means a large frozen reasoning model.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Text-to-SQL systems are increasingly used where a wrong answer is costly, so deciding when to answer
and when to abstain matters as much as raw accuracy. That decision rests on a calibrated estimate of
whether a generated query is correct. Most of the uncertainty-quantification (UQ) literature relies
on black-box statistical signals such as sampling agreement
\citep{wang2023selfconsistency}, semantic entropy \citep{kuhn2023semantic,farquhar2024semantic}, and
structural frequency, which are appealing because they need no extra model. We ask a direct question:
on hard text-to-SQL, which signals actually predict execution correctness, and by how much?

The contribution is an execution-grounded comparison, with confidence intervals, of the signals
available to a practitioner, together with the consequences for deployment.
\begin{enumerate}
\item A correctness ceiling for black-box statistical UQ on BIRD (roughly $0.61$ to $0.68$ \auroc{}),
shared by string, structural, and execution self-consistency, by structural priors and
schema-linking confidence, and, once compared against the strongest of these baselines, by white-box
log-probability.
\item Verification moves past the ceiling. LLM judges exceed it with bootstrapped confidence
intervals that exclude zero, and the result holds across two generators and two judge providers.
Judges from different providers make different errors, and combining them gives the best, and
best-calibrated, correctness signal we obtain ($0.82$ \auroc{}, $0.03$ ECE).
\item A selective-prediction frontier that the self-consistency baseline cannot match.
\item A study of trained verifiers, which do well in-distribution but fail to transfer across
schemas. We distinguish the per-deployment verifier from the universal one.
\end{enumerate}

Our scope is selective prediction rather than generation. We quantify and calibrate correctness to
decide whether to answer or abstain, and we do not iterate to improve the query, in contrast with the
self-correcting agents discussed in Section~\ref{sec:related}.

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}
Relevant lines of work include self-consistency and sampling-based UQ
\citep{wang2023selfconsistency}, semantic entropy via meaning clustering
\citep{kuhn2023semantic,farquhar2024semantic}, verbalized confidence, conformal and selective
generation \citep{geifman2017selective,angelopoulos2021ltt,angelopoulos2023conformalrisk},
sub-clause Platt scaling \citep{ramachandran2024calibrating}, and RTS conformal abstention over
schema-linking branch points \citep{somov2025rts}. Schema linking has also been addressed with
encoder-side graph models \citep{wang2020ratsql,cao2021lgesql}. LLM-as-judge is widely used for
open-ended evaluation; here we evaluate it specifically as a calibrated correctness predictor for
SQL, against black-box baselines, and we test whether judging can be trained and whether it
transfers. We use the Spider \citep{yu2018spider}, BIRD \citep{li2023bird}, and Spider~2.0
\citep{lei2025spider2} benchmarks for context.

A closely related line builds self-correcting or agentic text-to-SQL systems, in which the model
generates a query and then critiques or executes it and regenerates, often over several rounds
\citep{shinn2023reflexion,chen2023selfdebug,pourreza2023dinsql,wang2024macsql}. That work shares a
component with ours, since the critic in such a loop is an LLM verifier much like the judge we study.
The objective is different, though. A self-correcting agent uses the critic to drive iteration toward
a more accurate query, and reports end-task accuracy. We do not regenerate; we ask how well a signal
predicts correctness so that a system can answer or abstain under a risk target, and we report
calibration and risk--coverage rather than accuracy gains. The two are complementary: even after any
amount of self-correction, a system emits a final query and still needs a calibrated estimate of
whether to trust it, which is the question we study. We verify this directly in Section~4.2: one
round of self-correction barely changes accuracy and produces badly miscalibrated confidence
(ECE $0.50$), so it does not substitute for a calibrated verifier. We also note that typical self-correction relies
on execution feedback, whereas our verifier reasons over the question, schema, and SQL without
executing the candidate (and we find executability itself is uninformative for correctness,
Section~4.1).

\section{Setup}
\textit{Benchmark and generation.} We use BIRD dev \citep{li2023bird}, which has 11 databases with
data dictionaries and external-knowledge ``evidence.'' We generate SQL with \texttt{gpt-4o-mini}
($K=8$ samples per question, temperature $0.7$, schema and evidence in the prompt) for an
800-question slice across 8 databases, and we execute every sample against the real SQLite databases
to get ground-truth correctness. Modal-query execution accuracy is $0.451$, a hard regime with ample
headroom for UQ. A second generator, \texttt{gpt-4.1-mini} (accuracy $0.522$), is used for the
breadth check.

\textit{Signals.} The black-box statistical signals are string self-consistency (the largest
identical-string cluster), structural self-consistency (canonical-structure cluster), execution
self-consistency (result-set cluster), and schema-linking confidence (per-node posteriors over the
generated query, from a companion study). The logic-aware signals are the mean sequence
log-probability of the modal query (white-box) and an LLM verifier shown the question, evidence,
schema, and candidate SQL. The verifiers are \texttt{gpt-4o-mini} and \texttt{gpt-4o}, which report
$P(\text{correct})$ from the YES/NO first-token logits, and Claude-Sonnet-4.6, used as an
independent-provider judge with a verbalized 0--100 probability since Anthropic does not expose
token logprobs.

\textit{Metrics.} We report tie-robust \auroc{} for correctness, a cross-fit logistic combination
(parity split) with 2{,}000-sample bootstrap confidence intervals on the \auroc{} delta, expected
calibration error (ECE) \citep{guo2017calibration}, and risk--coverage with a distribution-free
(Bonferroni-over-grid, $\delta=0.1$) certificate \citep{angelopoulos2021ltt} alongside an empirical
frontier. All generation and verification is safe-by-default, with cost estimates and caching; total
API spend is about \$1.6.

\section{Results}

\subsection{The black-box correctness ceiling, and where white-box logprob sits}
\begin{table}[h]\centering
\begin{tabular}{lr}
\toprule
signal (alone) & \auroc{} for correctness \\
\midrule
string self-consistency (largest identical-string cluster) & 0.675 \\
structural self-consistency (largest canonical-structure cluster) & 0.619 \\
execution self-consistency (largest result-set cluster) & 0.613 \\
log-probability (white-box, mean sequence logprob) & 0.669 \\
schema-linking graph confidence & 0.553 \\
``does it execute / return rows'' & 0.500 (chance) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The black-box statistical signals plateau between about $0.61$ and $0.68$ \auroc{}, with string
self-consistency the strongest at $0.675$. White-box log-probability does not exceed this ceiling
($0.669$); added to string self-consistency it gives $+0.005$ (CI $[-0.003, +0.012]$), which is not
significant. Log-probability did separate confident-wrong queries on saturated single-table data,
but on hard multi-table BIRD it is no better than sampling agreement. Wrong queries also execute and
return rows without trouble, so executability is uninformative.

\subsection{Verification moves past the ceiling}
We combine each signal with the strongest baseline (string self-consistency) using a cross-fit
logistic.
\begin{table}[h]\centering
\begin{tabular}{lrr}
\toprule
signal & \auroc{} alone & combined $\Delta$ over string SC (95\% CI) \\
\midrule
log-probability (white-box) & 0.669 & $+0.005\ [-0.003, +0.012]$ (n.s.) \\
LLM verifier (\texttt{gpt-4o-mini}) & 0.724 & --- \\
LLM verifier (\texttt{gpt-4o}) & 0.770 & $+0.079\ [+0.054, +0.105]$ \\
LLM verifier (Claude-Sonnet-4.6) & 0.776 & $+0.131\ [+0.101, +0.163]$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Only the verifiers move past the ceiling, with confidence intervals that exclude zero. The signal
that works is the one that is not blind to query logic: a model that can reason about whether the
aggregation, filters, and joins answer the question. Log-probability adds nothing over the strongest
sampling baseline here.

\textit{A self-correction baseline.} Self-correcting agents are an alternative way to use a critic,
so we also run one reflection round: each modal query is fed back to gpt-4o-mini, which reviews it,
revises it if needed, and reports a confidence. On this slice the revision improves execution
accuracy only marginally ($0.451$ to $0.468$), and the model's self-reported confidence is not usable
for abstention. It assigns at least $0.9$ confidence to $99\%$ of queries (mean $0.97$), giving
\auroc{} $0.637$ and ECE $0.501$ for the revised query's correctness. A single round of
self-correction therefore neither closes the accuracy gap nor yields a calibrated trust signal, so an
external verifier remains necessary and the abstention question is orthogonal to iteration.

\subsection{Robustness: cross-provider judges and a second generator}
A verifier from the generator's own family might merely echo it, so we also test an independent
provider. Claude-Sonnet-4.6, judging GPT-4o-mini's SQL, is the strongest single judge
($0.776$ versus $0.770$), and the two judges' scores correlate at only $r=0.43$, so they make
genuinely different errors. A two-provider ensemble (GPT-4o and Claude) reaches $0.822$ \auroc{}
($\Delta$ over GPT-4o alone of $+0.052$, CI $[+0.032, +0.073]$), the strongest correctness signal we
obtain, and it is well-calibrated (ECE $0.031$; Section~\ref{sec:cal}). This rules out a
self-agreement artifact and suggests that combining independent reasoning judges, rather than using a
single larger one, is the most reliable approach. The OpenAI judges use YES/NO logits while Claude
uses a verbalized probability; a method-matched OpenAI verbal judge also clears the ceiling at
$0.709$, so the gap is not an elicitation artifact.

The pattern is not specific to gpt-4o-mini. Regenerating the slice with \texttt{gpt-4.1-mini}
(accuracy $0.522$, a stronger generator) and judging with the same gpt-4o-mini verifier reproduces
it.
\begin{table}[h]\centering
\begin{tabular}{lrrrr}
\toprule
generator & accuracy & string SC & verifier & combined $\Delta$ over SC (95\% CI) \\
\midrule
gpt-4o-mini & 0.451 & 0.675 & 0.724 & $+0.059\ [+0.032, +0.087]$ \\
gpt-4.1-mini & 0.522 & 0.641 & 0.677 & $+0.033\ [+0.007, +0.061]$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

For both generators, self-consistency sits at the ceiling and the verifier exceeds it, with a
combined delta whose interval excludes zero. The margin is smaller for gpt-4.1-mini, which is
expected: there the judge (gpt-4o-mini) is weaker than the generator and so catches fewer of its
subtler errors. A judge at least as strong as the generator should widen the gap.

\subsection{Can the verifier be trained? In-distribution yes, transfer no}
\label{sec:trained}
We fine-tune verifiers on (question, evidence, schema, SQL) $\rightarrow$ correct, using 6{,}400
execution-labeled pairs, evaluated leave-one-database-out (LODO). We compare an encoder classifier
and a generative LLM judge (LoRA), along with an un-fine-tuned baseline.
\begin{table}[h]\centering
\begin{tabular}{lrr}
\toprule
verifier & in-distribution \auroc{} & leave-one-DB-out (transfer) \\
\midrule
cheap feature classifier (embeddings, logprob, SC) & 0.768 & 0.661 \\
fine-tuned encoder, RoBERTa-base & 0.778 & --- \\
fine-tuned encoder, ModernBERT-base (full schema) & 0.785 & 0.670 \\
generative judge, Qwen2.5-1.5B, zero-shot & 0.651 & 0.553 \\
generative judge, Qwen2.5-1.5B, LoRA fine-tuned & 0.766 & 0.659 \\
frozen GPT-4o judge (zero-shot) & --- & 0.770 (already a transfer number) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Fine-tuning helps in-distribution; it lifts the small generative judge by $+0.115$ in-distribution
and $+0.106$ on transfer over its zero-shot baseline. It does not transfer well, though. Every
fine-tuned verifier, encoder or generative, converges to about $0.77$ in-distribution and about
$0.66$ across unseen schemas, with the worst results on the most domain-specific held-out databases.
Long context (ModernBERT) and a generative architecture both fail to close this gap, which suggests
the models are learning schema surface form. The frozen large judge is the exception: GPT-4o's
$0.77$ is already its cross-schema number, and the small base model's near-chance zero-shot transfer
($0.553$) shows why. Transfer here seems to follow model scale and reasoning rather than fine-tuning.
Per database (Figure~\ref{fig:lodo}), the frozen GPT-4o judge leads on every held-out schema (mean
$0.710$), above both fine-tuned verifiers ($0.670$ and $0.659$).

An input ablation of the frozen judge is consistent with this reading. Shown only the question and
SQL, the judge scores $0.692$; adding the schema changes nothing ($0.688$, a change of $-0.004$); and
only evidence helps ($+0.031$, to $0.724$). The frozen verifier reasons about whether the query
answers the question rather than looking up schema content, which is why it generalizes, and which is
the opposite of what the fine-tuned verifiers do.

In short, there are two distinct problems. A per-deployment verifier, trained on a database's own
schemas, is a good and inexpensive option at about $0.77$ to $0.79$. A universal verifier, on this
evidence, is a large frozen reasoning model: no small fine-tuned verifier ($\le 1.5$B) beats
GPT-4o's $0.77$ on transfer.

The per-deployment case has a concrete payoff for abstention. Used as the selective-prediction score,
a cheap trained verifier (a logistic over question/SQL embeddings, log-probability, and
self-consistency, with no LLM call at inference) yields a risk--coverage frontier competitive with
the frozen GPT-4o judge and far above self-consistency. At a $20\%$ risk target it answers $21\%$ of
questions, against $9\%$ for the frozen judge and $1\%$ for self-consistency (area under the
risk--coverage curve $0.340$, $0.355$, and $0.413$ respectively; lower is better). So when the
deployment owns its schemas, a trained verifier is not only accurate in-distribution but also the
cheaper way to get risk-controlled abstention; the fine-tuned encoders, which score higher
in-distribution still, would do at least as well.

\subsection{Calibration and selective prediction}
\label{sec:cal}
The raw signals are over-confident, while the cross-fit ensemble is well-calibrated and can be used
directly as $P(\text{correct})$ (Figure~\ref{fig:reliab}).
\begin{table}[h]\centering
\begin{tabular}{lrr}
\toprule
score & \auroc{} & ECE \\
\midrule
string self-consistency & 0.675 & 0.212 \\
verifier (GPT-4o, raw $P$) & 0.770 & 0.319 \\
verifier (Claude, raw $P$) & 0.776 & 0.210 \\
two-provider ensemble (cross-fit) & 0.822 & 0.031 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

For selective prediction, we report an empirical risk--coverage frontier (Figure~\ref{fig:rc}) with
the threshold calibrated on a held-out half.
\begin{table}[h]\centering
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
target risk & self-consistency & all signals \\
\midrule
0.20 & none (no valid subset) & 9\% coverage at 14\% risk \\
0.30 & none & 27\% coverage at 24\% risk \\
0.40 & none & 58\% coverage at 38\% risk \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}
Self-consistency cannot form a low-risk subset at any threshold, whereas the logic-aware combination
gives useful empirical abstention frontiers. The distribution-free certificate is conservative under
the regime's low base accuracy ($0.45$): only ``all signals at $\alpha=0.40$'' certifies (16\%
coverage, 22\% risk). A tighter guarantee would need a stronger generator or more calibration data
rather than a better signal, so we report the empirical frontier as the practical result and the
certificate as a conservative lower bound.

\section{Discussion}
Correctness uncertainty for text-to-SQL is tractable, but only with signals that engage the query's
logic. The popular black-box statistical signals are a ceiling rather than a solution on hard
multi-table data. Two practical recipes follow. For a fixed deployment, a cheap trained verifier
delivers about $0.78$ at negligible inference cost. For an open setting, a reasoning judge is the
better choice, since the frozen LLM already transfers at $0.77$ and combining independent providers
is the most reliable and best-calibrated option.

\section{Limitations}
The generators are within one provider; we show the effect for two models (Section~4.3), and a judge
stronger than the generator would likely widen the gpt-4.1-mini margin. The PAC certificate is loose
under low base accuracy. Whether a universal trained verifier is possible at larger scale (7B and up)
remains open; the only verifier that generalizes across schemas in our experiments is the large
frozen judge. Planned additions include an open-weight generator, a second benchmark (Spider), and a
third judge from another provider.

\section{Conclusion}
On hard text-to-SQL, what predicts correctness is not sampling agreement, structure, or even
white-box log-probability, all of which plateau between about $0.61$ and $0.68$ \auroc{}. It is
verification: an LLM judge that reasons about whether the query answers the question.
Independent-provider judges make different errors and ensemble to $0.82$ \auroc{} with calibrated
probabilities, which enables empirical abstention that the sampling baseline cannot provide.
Verifiers can be trained, but a fine-tuned model learns schema surface form and fails to transfer, so
a universal verifier needs to generalize by reasoning. The map itself is the contribution: it
indicates which signal to pay for, and when.
