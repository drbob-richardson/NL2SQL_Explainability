\begin{abstract}
When should we trust an LLM's SQL? We study, on equal footing and with bootstrapped confidence
intervals, which signals predict whether a generated query is \emph{execution-correct} on hard
multi-table text-to-SQL (BIRD). We find a sharp dichotomy. \textbf{Black-box statistical signals
plateau:} string, structural, and execution self-consistency, structural priors, and semantic
schema-linking confidence all sit at \auroc{} $\approx 0.61$--$0.68$ (string self-consistency,
$0.675$, is the strongest), and \textbf{white-box log-probability does not exceed them} ($0.67$; no
significant gain when combined with the best baseline). \textbf{What breaks the ceiling is
verification:} an LLM judge scores $0.72$ (GPT-4o-mini) to $0.78$ (Claude), and because
independent-provider judges make \emph{different} errors (GPT-4o vs.\ Claude score correlation
$0.43$) a two-provider ensemble reaches \auroc{} $\mathbf{0.82}$ with a well-calibrated correctness
probability (ECE $0.03$), supporting useful abstention frontiers (e.g., $27\%$ coverage at $24\%$
selective risk) where self-consistency yields no valid low-risk subset---though distribution-free
certificates stay conservative under the regime's low base accuracy. The verifier dichotomy
replicates across two generators and two judge providers. We further ask whether the verifier can be
\emph{trained}: fine-tuned verifiers---encoder and generative alike---reach in-distribution \auroc{}
$\approx 0.77$--$0.79$ (matching the frozen GPT-4o judge) but \textbf{do not transfer}, dropping to
$\approx 0.66$ across unseen schemas. Cross-schema transfer tracks \textbf{model scale and
reasoning}, not fine-tuning. The practical upshot: correctness UQ for text-to-SQL lives in
\emph{reasoning-based} signals; a fine-tuned verifier is an excellent \emph{per-deployment} tool,
while a \emph{universal} verifier requires a model that generalizes across schemas---so far, a large
frozen reasoning judge.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Text-to-SQL systems are deployed where wrong answers are costly, so the decision \emph{``answer or
abstain''} matters as much as raw accuracy. That decision needs a calibrated estimate of whether a
generated query is correct. The uncertainty-quantification (UQ) literature is dominated by
\textbf{black-box statistical} signals---sampling agreement \citep{wang2023selfconsistency},
semantic entropy \citep{kuhn2023semantic,farquhar2024semantic}, structural frequency---that are
attractive because they need no extra model. We ask directly: \textbf{on hard text-to-SQL, which
signals actually predict execution correctness, and by how much?}

Our contribution is a careful, execution-grounded \emph{map} with confidence intervals, plus the
deployment consequences:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{A correctness ceiling for black-box statistical UQ} (\auroc{} $\approx 0.61$--$0.68$ on
BIRD), shared by string/structural/execution self-consistency, structural priors, schema-linking
confidence, and---once measured against the \emph{strongest} baseline---white-box log-probability.
\item \textbf{Verification breaks it}: LLM judges clearly exceed the ceiling with bootstrapped CIs
excluding zero, replicating across \emph{two generators} and \emph{two judge providers};
independent-provider judges make different errors and \textbf{ensemble to the best, calibrated
correctness signal} (\auroc{} $0.82$, ECE $0.03$).
\item \textbf{A selective-prediction frontier}: the combined score supports empirical abstention the
self-consistency baseline cannot.
\item \textbf{A trained-verifier study}: fine-tuned verifiers win in-distribution but \textbf{fail to
transfer} across schemas; we separate the \emph{per-deployment} from the \emph{universal} verifier
problem.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related work}
Self-consistency and sampling-based UQ \citep{wang2023selfconsistency}; semantic entropy via meaning
clustering \citep{kuhn2023semantic,farquhar2024semantic}; verbalized confidence; conformal/selective
generation \citep{geifman2017selective,angelopoulos2021ltt,angelopoulos2023conformalrisk};
sub-clause Platt scaling \citep{ramachandran2024calibrating}; RTS conformal abstention over
schema-linking branch points \citep{somov2025rts}. Schema linking has been addressed with encoder-side
graph models \citep{wang2020ratsql,cao2021lgesql}. LLM-as-judge is widely used for open-ended
evaluation; we evaluate it specifically as a \emph{calibrated correctness predictor for SQL} against
black-box baselines, and test whether judging can be \emph{trained} and whether it \emph{transfers}.
Benchmarks: Spider \citep{yu2018spider}, BIRD \citep{li2023bird}, Spider~2.0 \citep{lei2025spider2}.

\section{Setup}
\textbf{Benchmark and generation.} BIRD dev \citep{li2023bird} (11 databases with data dictionaries
and external-knowledge ``evidence''). We generate SQL with \texttt{gpt-4o-mini} ($K=8$ samples per
question, temperature $0.7$, schema $+$ evidence in the prompt) for an 800-question slice across 8
databases, and execute every sample against the real SQLite databases for ground-truth correctness.
Modal-query execution accuracy is $0.451$---a genuinely hard regime with ample headroom for UQ. A
second generator, \texttt{gpt-4.1-mini} (accuracy $0.522$), is used for the breadth check.

\textbf{Signals.} \emph{Black-box statistical:} string self-consistency (largest identical-string
cluster), structural self-consistency (canonical-structure cluster), execution self-consistency
(result-set cluster), and schema-linking confidence (per-node posteriors over the generated query,
from a companion study). \emph{Logic-aware:} mean sequence \textbf{log-probability} of the modal
query (white-box); an \textbf{LLM verifier} shown (question, evidence, schema, candidate SQL)---
\texttt{gpt-4o-mini} and \texttt{gpt-4o} (P from YES/NO first-token logits), and
\textbf{Claude-Sonnet-4.6} as an independent-provider judge (a verbalized 0--100 probability, since
Anthropic exposes no token logprobs).

\textbf{Metrics.} Tie-robust \auroc{} for correctness; cross-fit logistic combination (parity split)
with 2{,}000-sample bootstrap CIs on the \auroc{} delta; expected calibration error (ECE)
\citep{guo2017calibration}; risk--coverage with a distribution-free (Bonferroni-over-grid,
$\delta=0.1$) certificate \citep{angelopoulos2021ltt} and an empirical frontier. All
generation/verification is safe-by-default (cost estimate, caching); total API spend $\approx \$1.6$.

\section{Results}

\subsection{The black-box correctness ceiling (and where white-box logprob sits)}
\begin{table}[h]\centering
\begin{tabular}{lr}
\toprule
signal (alone) & \auroc{} for correctness \\
\midrule
\textbf{string self-consistency} (largest identical-string cluster) & \textbf{0.675} \\
structural self-consistency (largest canonical-structure cluster) & 0.619 \\
execution self-consistency (largest result-set cluster) & 0.613 \\
log-probability (white-box, mean sequence logprob) & 0.669 \\
schema-linking graph confidence & 0.553 \\
``does it execute / return rows'' & 0.500 (chance) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The black-box statistical signals plateau at \auroc{} $\approx 0.61$--$0.68$; \emph{string}
self-consistency is the strongest (0.675). Crucially, \textbf{white-box log-probability does not
exceed this ceiling} (0.669): added to string self-consistency it gives $+0.005$ (CI
$[-0.003, +0.012]$, not significant). Log-probability separated confident-wrong queries on
\emph{saturated single-table} data, but on hard multi-table BIRD it is no better than sampling
agreement. Wrong queries also execute and return rows just fine, so executability is chance.

\subsection{Verification breaks the ceiling}
We combine each signal with the strongest baseline (string self-consistency) via cross-fit logistic.
\begin{table}[h]\centering
\begin{tabular}{lrr}
\toprule
signal & \auroc{} alone & combined $\Delta$ over string SC (95\% CI) \\
\midrule
log-probability (white-box) & 0.669 & $+0.005\ [-0.003, +0.012]$ (n.s.) \\
LLM verifier (\texttt{gpt-4o-mini}) & 0.724 & --- \\
LLM verifier (\texttt{gpt-4o}) & 0.770 & $+0.079\ [+0.054, +0.105]$ \\
LLM verifier (Claude-Sonnet-4.6) & \textbf{0.776} & $+0.131\ [+0.101, +0.163]$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Only the verifiers break the ceiling, with CIs excluding zero. The signal that works is the one not
blind to query logic: a model that can \emph{reason} about whether the aggregation, filters, and
joins answer the question. Log-probability adds nothing over the strongest sampling baseline here.

\subsection{Robustness: cross-provider judges and a second generator}
A verifier from the generator's own family could merely echo it, so we test an \textbf{independent
provider}. Claude-Sonnet-4.6 (judging GPT-4o-mini's SQL) is the \emph{strongest single judge}
($0.776 > 0.770$), and the two judges' scores correlate only $r=0.43$---they make genuinely different
errors. As a result a \textbf{two-provider ensemble (GPT-4o $+$ Claude) reaches \auroc{} 0.822}
($\Delta$ over GPT-4o alone $+0.052$, CI $[+0.032, +0.073]$), the strongest correctness signal we
obtain, and is well-calibrated (ECE 0.031; \S\ref{sec:cal}). This rules out the self-agreement
artifact and shows that combining independent reasoning judges---not a single bigger one---is the
most reliable correctness signal. (The OpenAI judges use YES/NO logits and Claude a verbalized
probability; a method-matched OpenAI verbal judge also clears the ceiling at 0.709.)

\textbf{A second generator.} The dichotomy is not unique to gpt-4o-mini. Regenerating the slice with
\texttt{gpt-4.1-mini} (accuracy 0.522, a stronger generator), judged by the same gpt-4o-mini verifier:
\begin{table}[h]\centering
\begin{tabular}{lrrrr}
\toprule
generator & accuracy & string SC & verifier & combined $\Delta$ over SC (95\% CI) \\
\midrule
gpt-4o-mini & 0.451 & 0.675 & 0.724 & $+0.059\ [+0.032, +0.087]$ \\
gpt-4.1-mini & 0.522 & 0.641 & 0.677 & $+0.033\ [+0.007, +0.061]$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

For both generators, self-consistency sits at the ceiling and the verifier exceeds it with a combined
delta whose CI excludes zero. The margin is smaller for gpt-4.1-mini---expected, since there the judge
(gpt-4o-mini) is \emph{weaker} than the generator and catches fewer of its subtler errors; a judge at
least as strong as the generator should widen it.

\subsection{Can the verifier be trained? In-distribution yes, transfer no}
\label{sec:trained}
We fine-tune verifiers on (question $+$ evidence $+$ schema $+$ SQL $\rightarrow$ correct), 6{,}400
execution-labeled pairs, evaluated leave-one-database-out (LODO), across an encoder classifier and a
generative LLM judge (LoRA), plus an un-fine-tuned baseline.
\begin{table}[h]\centering
\begin{tabular}{lrr}
\toprule
verifier & in-distribution \auroc{} & leave-one-DB-out (transfer) \\
\midrule
cheap feature classifier (embeddings $+$ logprob $+$ SC) & 0.768 & 0.661 \\
fine-tuned encoder, RoBERTa-base & 0.778 & --- \\
fine-tuned encoder, ModernBERT-base (full schema) & 0.785 & 0.670 \\
generative judge, Qwen2.5-1.5B --- zero-shot & 0.651 & 0.553 \\
generative judge, Qwen2.5-1.5B --- LoRA fine-tuned & 0.766 & 0.659 \\
\emph{frozen GPT-4o judge (zero-shot)} & --- & \emph{0.770 (already a transfer number)} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Fine-tuning works (it lifts the small generative judge $+0.115$ in-distribution and $+0.106$ on
transfer over its zero-shot baseline), \textbf{but it does not transfer}: every fine-tuned verifier,
encoder or generative, converges to $\approx 0.77$ in-distribution and $\approx 0.66$ across unseen
schemas, worst on the most domain-specific held-out databases. Long context (ModernBERT) and a
generative architecture both fail to close it; the models memorize schema surface form. The frozen
large judge is the exception---GPT-4o's $0.77$ \emph{is} its cross-schema number, and the small base
model's near-chance zero-shot transfer ($0.553$) shows why: transfer tracks \textbf{model scale and
reasoning}, not fine-tuning. Per database (Fig.~\ref{fig:lodo}), the frozen GPT-4o judge leads on
every held-out schema (mean $0.710$) above both fine-tuned verifiers ($0.670$, $0.659$).

\textbf{What the verifier uses.} An input ablation of the frozen judge confirms the mechanism: shown
only the question and SQL it scores $0.692$; adding the schema changes nothing ($0.688$,
$\Delta -0.004$); only evidence helps ($+0.031 \rightarrow 0.724$). The frozen verifier reasons about
whether the query answers the question rather than looking up schema content---precisely why it
generalizes, and the mirror image of the fine-tuned verifiers.

\textbf{Consequence.} A \emph{per-deployment} verifier (trained on a database's own schemas) is an
excellent, near-zero-inference-cost option at $\approx 0.77$--$0.79$. A \emph{universal} verifier, on
this evidence, is a large frozen reasoning model: no small fine-tuned verifier ($\le 1.5$B) beats
GPT-4o's $0.77$ on transfer.

\subsection{Calibration and selective prediction}
\label{sec:cal}
\textbf{Calibration.} The raw signals are over-confident; the cross-fit ensemble is well-calibrated
and usable directly as $P(\text{correct})$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:reliab}).
\begin{table}[h]\centering
\begin{tabular}{lrr}
\toprule
score & \auroc{} & ECE \\
\midrule
string self-consistency & 0.675 & 0.212 \\
verifier (GPT-4o, raw P) & 0.770 & 0.319 \\
verifier (Claude, raw P) & 0.776 & 0.210 \\
\textbf{two-provider ensemble (cross-fit)} & \textbf{0.822} & \textbf{0.031} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\textbf{Selective prediction.} Empirical risk--coverage (Fig.~\ref{fig:rc}); threshold calibrated on
a held-out half:
\begin{table}[h]\centering
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
target risk & self-consistency & all signals \\
\midrule
0.20 & --- (no valid subset) & 9\% coverage @ 14\% risk \\
0.30 & --- & 27\% coverage @ 24\% risk \\
0.40 & --- & 58\% coverage @ 38\% risk \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}
Self-consistency cannot form a low-risk subset at any threshold; the logic-aware combination yields
useful empirical abstention frontiers. The distribution-free PAC certificate is conservative under
the regime's low base accuracy (0.45)---only ``all signals at $\alpha=0.40$'' certifies (16\%
coverage / 22\% risk)---so a tight guarantee needs a stronger generator or more calibration data, not
a better signal. We report the empirical frontier as the practical result and the certificate as a
conservative lower bound.

\section{Discussion}
Correctness UQ for text-to-SQL is tractable, but only with signals that engage the query's
\emph{logic}. The popular black-box statistical signals are a ceiling, not a solution, on hard
multi-table data. Two recipes follow: (i) for a fixed deployment, a cheap trained verifier delivers
$\approx 0.78$ at negligible inference cost; (ii) for an open setting, use a reasoning judge---the
frozen LLM already transfers at $0.77$, and combining independent providers is the most reliable and
best-calibrated option.

\section{Limitations}
Generators are within one provider (two models shown, \S4.3); a stronger judge than the generator
would widen the gpt-4.1-mini margin. PAC certificates are loose under low base accuracy. A
\emph{universal} trained verifier remains open at the large-model ($\ge 7$B) scale; the only verifier
that generalizes across schemas so far is the large frozen judge. Planned: an open-weight generator,
a second benchmark (Spider), and a cross-\emph{provider} third judge.

\section{Conclusion}
On hard text-to-SQL, \emph{what predicts correctness} is not sampling agreement, structure, or even
white-box log-probability---those plateau at \auroc{} $\approx 0.61$--$0.68$---but \textbf{verification}:
an LLM judge that reasons about whether the query answers the question. Independent-provider judges
make different errors and ensemble to \auroc{} $0.82$ with calibrated probabilities, enabling
empirical abstention the sampling baseline cannot. Verifiers can be trained, but a fine-tuned model
overfits schemas and fails to transfer, so a \emph{universal} verifier must generalize by reasoning.
The map is the contribution: it tells practitioners which signal to pay for, and when.
